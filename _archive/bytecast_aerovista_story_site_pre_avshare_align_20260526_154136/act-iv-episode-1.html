Act IV - Episode 1

Why I Stopped Trusting Rented Infrastructure

And realize your entire life is running on rented infrastructure.

Like most people do. A tool for everything. A platform for everything. A subscription for everything.
Jump to content

Opening Sequence

At first…

I loved software.

Like most people do.

It felt magical.

A tool for everything.

A platform for everything.

A subscription for everything.

Need storage

Buy software.

Need communication

Buy software.

Need project management

Buy software.

Need marketing

Buy software.

Need automation

Buy software.

Need design

Buy software.

Need collaboration

Buy software.

Need analytics

Buy software.

Need AI

Buy software.

At first…

that feels efficient.

Until you zoom out.

And realize your entire life is running on rented infrastructure.

That realization hit me hard.

Because I had already spent years watching fragile systems fail.

Corporate systems.

Employment systems.

Operational systems.

And now I was watching the same thing happen digitally.

Pricing changes.

Feature removals.

Platform shutdowns.

Terms of service changes.

API restrictions.

Vendor lock-in.

Subscription stacking.

Tools becoming dramatically more expensive once dependency is established.

That pattern felt very familiar.

And very dangerous.

Because if your business relies entirely on tools you do not control…

you do not fully own your business.

That thought stayed with me.

And it kept getting louder.

Especially as I built more projects.

More apps.

More products.

More workflows.

More media systems.

More operational infrastructure.

The dependency stack kept growing.

And eventually I asked a very uncomfortable question.

How much of my future am I renting

That question changed everything.

Because the honest answer was

too much.

Way too much.

And once I saw that clearly…

I couldn’t go back to pretending it was normal.

I started seeing subscriptions differently.

Some were worth it.

Many were convenience traps.

I started seeing platforms differently.

Some created leverage.

Others created dependency disguised as ease.

I started seeing cloud infrastructure differently.

Useful

Absolutely.

Permanent dependency

That felt dangerous.

And this wasn’t about becoming anti-technology.

It was the opposite.

I loved technology enough to want deeper ownership.

That distinction matters.

A lot.

I didn’t want less capability.

I wanted more control.

More flexibility.

More resilience.

More optionality.

And I started asking bigger questions.

What should be owned

What should be rented

What should be local

What should be cloud-based

What creates unnecessary dependency

What creates resilience

Those questions completely changed my architecture philosophy.

And they eventually pushed me toward something that looked ridiculous to some people.

Building my own infrastructure.

At home.

In my own environment.

On systems I controlled.

That decision changed everything.

Because once I started building owned infrastructure…

my house stopped feeling like just a house.

And started feeling like the early version of something much bigger.

That’s where NXCore begins.

And that story gets very interesting.

Key Lessons

Lesson 1

convenience can create dangerous dependency

Lesson 2

subscription stacking creates hidden fragility

Lesson 3

ownership creates optionality

Lesson 4

cloud services should be strategic, not default

Lesson 5

resilience requires intentional infrastructure decisions

Why This Mattered Later

This directly led to

NXCore private hosting infrastructure self-hosted services reduced SaaS dependency long-term resilience architecture hybrid cloud strategy

This episode explains the philosophy behind your infrastructure decisions.

Closing Hook

I got tired of renting my future.

So I started building one I could actually control.

And that started in my own home.
